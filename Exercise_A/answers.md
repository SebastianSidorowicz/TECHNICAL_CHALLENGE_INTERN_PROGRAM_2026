## 1. Describe the most relevant learnings from programming-related subjects during your career.

The learning process was not only about languages, but also about the evolution of thinking:

**Programming Fundamentals and Data Structures.**  
Learning when to use a Hashmap over a list based on the complexity of the task was the first leap from “programmer” to “analyst,” understanding that not everything is Imperative or Object-Oriented, but that depending on the task, there are more coherent ways to approach it.

**Design and Architecture.**  
The most valuable learning here is Design Patterns (SOLID, GRASP, GoF), understanding that code must be maintainable and scalable. Moving from writing “scripts” to designing “systems” where decoupling is the rule.

**Data Management and Concurrency.**  
The ability to integrate applications with relational and NoSQL database engines, managing transactions and understanding the issues of concurrency and distributed systems.

**Software quality through testing.**  
Truly understand what the customer needs and how to validate their needs, ensuring that the system meets them so that the product complies with the user's functional and non-functional requirements, expectations and standards.

## 2. Describe a situation you had to resolve with a teammate from a study or work group.

In the subject of software engineering and quality, the problem arose that the working groups were too large (10 people) and everyone had to participate in the tasks, as it was a requirement that they would later be verified on GitHub. Given the size of the group, the main problem was that it was always “the same people who did the work or left no tasks for the others.”

After a few weeks and some discomfort, I decided to create and manage a team in Jira. It turned out to be quite practical for everyone, as each person was responsible for their own tasks and everyone could see on the board how they were progressing and what they had to do, which improved communication within the group.

Instead of sending an isolated message to the group asking if anyone had modeled the objects to insert into the database, the person assigned to the task was asked directly how it was going, if they needed help, or if it was visible on the board that it was already done, then they could continue with their tasks that depended on it.

It was very gratifying that it worked. We were able to apply the agile methodologies we were learning in a real environment, thanks to the willingness of the whole team. Not only did it improve communication, but it also sped up the completion of tasks.

*(As an additional comment, from that day on, they jokingly nicknamed me the team's Scrum Master).*
